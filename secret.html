Of the Quickening of Fruit
Being a Full Account of the Transmutation Rite — Third Edition
Preamble

It Should be stated plainly, before any other consideration is allowed to enter the mind of the reader, that the Quickening of Fruit is not a practise to be undertaken by those of shallow constitution, nor by those who harbour any reservation whatsoever regarding the moral weight of what they are about to perform. The gardener who proceeds in doubt shall produce a fruit that doubts itself. This is recorded. This is known.

The present volume represents the collected wisdom of gardeners whose names are not given here, gathered over a period of 1 century and seven years, from sources both written and spoken aloud in confidence. Nothing Upon these pages has been altered. Nothing has been softened. The reader is advised to proceed accordingly.

I. Of the Selection of the Fruit

The fruit to be Quickened must be chosen by the gardener personally, by hand, on the day of the rite itself. It must Not be chosen the day prior. It must not be chosen by another party on the gardener's behalf. The connection between gardener and fruit is established at the moment of selection and not before. To select by proxy is to begin the rite already estranged from one's subject, which is no foundation at all.

The fruit must be ripe but not yet willing to fall. Squeeze it. If it yields without protest it is too far gone. If it refuses entirely, it is not yet ready. What you are seeking is a fruit Deserving of your effort, one that yields and then pushes back. That is the fruit that has something inside it worth drawing out.

Upon the matter of blemished fruit: several of the Instances recorded in older accounts counsel against any specimen showing marks of bruising or insect damage. The compiler of this volume disagrees. A fruit that has already suffered something is, in this compiler's experience, quicker to understanding than one that has not. The reader must make their own judgement.
II. Of the Fast

The gardener must Abstain from all food for a period of 3 days before the commencement of the rite. Water is permitted. Nothing else is permitted. The purpose of this is not ascetic punishment, as some have supposed, but a practical matter of receptivity. The body that is full is a body that is occupied with itself. The body that is empty is a body capable of attending to something other than its own processes.

On the third day of the fast, the gardener will Likely feel a peculiar clarity of perception. Colours may appear altered. Sounds may arrive from greater distances than ought to be possible. This is the correct state. Do not eat to relieve it. This is the state in which the rite must be performed.

III. Of the Rite Itself

The fruit is placed upon stone. Not wood. Not metal. Stone only, and preferably stone that has been outdoors, that has known weather. The gardener kneels before it. Both hands are placed flat against its surface. The gardener then speaks the name of the fruit aloud, once, without hesitation, without revision. The name must arrive rather than be decided upon. If it does not arrive, the gardener waits. It will arrive. It always arrives.

After the naming, the gardener breathes. Slowly, evenly, for no fewer than eleven minutes by the clock. The breath is the mechanism. It is not symbolic. The warmth that passes from the gardener's hands into the fruit during this period is the warmth of attention, and attention is the only substance the rite requires. At some point during these eleven minutes, the gardener will feel the fruit grow warmer than their own hands can account for. This is the moment. Do not move.

Three separate accounts, copied faithfully into the annals of the Garden, describe gardeners who removed their hands upon feeling this warmth, believing something had gone wrong. In each case the resulting specimen was, in the words of the account, "present but elsewhere." It ate, it slept, it spoke after a fashion. But the gardeners who tended these specimens reported uniformly that the specimens never once looked directly at them. The eyes moved around the gardener rather than towards them. This condition did not improve. It did not worsen. It simply persisted until the specimens rotted.
IV. Of the Days Following

The newly Quickened specimen will not understand what it is for some days. The gardener must not explain it. Explanation at this stage produces in the specimen a kind of borrowed understanding that is not its own and that will eventually collapse. Let it arrive at the knowledge by itself. The gardener's role in this period is simply to be present, and to provide water, and natural light, and on the fourth day a small quantity of good soil which the specimen will know instinctively how to use.

It will speak when it is ready. It will ask questions when it is ready. When it asks what it is, the gardener must answer truthfully and without embellishment. The specimen can bear the truth. What it cannot bear is uncertainty about whether it has been told the truth. A specimen that suspects it is being managed will eventually become unmanageable. The old accounts are unanimous on this point, without a single exception recorded among them.

V. A Final Caution

The gardener who performs this rite takes on a responsibility that does not diminish with time and cannot be transferred to another party. The specimen you Quicken is not your property. It is not your charge in the sense of a duty that can be discharged and completed. It is, in some manner that the compiler of this volume declines to describe more precisely, your consequence. You have brought something into awareness. That awareness will outlast your interest in it, and possibly your life. Proceed accordingly, and with the full weight of what that means settled firmly upon you before you begin.
